Lesson 02 · Reading

Three notes, and everything else is a distance

You don't read music by naming notes. You read it by knowing where you are.

First — two minutes on last lesson

No keyboard. Answer from memory. You scored 3 of 6 on these last time, which is the most useful number you've given me: it means you can do it with the keyboard in front of you but can't yet picture it. Those feel identical while practising and completely different a month later.

Why not "Every Good Boy Deserves Fudge"

You have probably met the mnemonics. They are a trap, and it's worth knowing why before we do anything else. A mnemonic makes you recite to find a note: you start at the bottom line and walk up a sentence. That is a counting strategy, and it fails for exactly the reason counting up from C failed you last lesson — it's fine at three notes a minute and useless at three notes a second. Good readers don't name notes at all. They see a shape relative to a place they already know (Music and Theory).

So we do the same thing that worked on the keyboard: memorise a few anchors, then read everything else as a distance from the nearest one.

The three landmarks Treble G — the line the treble clef curls around. That's why it's also called the G clef.
Bass F — the line the bass clef's two dots straddle. The F clef.
Middle C — the short ledger line floating between the two staves. It's the same key both times, written twice.

The clefs are not decoration. Each one is a label pointing at its own landmark. Once you see that, two of the three are free.

Steps and skips

Once you're on a landmark, the staff only moves two ways. Line to the very next space (or space to the next line) is a step — the adjacent white key. Line to the next line (or space to space) is a skip — you miss one white key out. That's the entire mechanic, and it maps directly onto your hand: a step is a neighbouring finger, a skip is one finger gap.

Careful A step on the staff means the next letter, not the next key. E to F is a step even though they're touching with no black key between them — and so is F to G, which has a black key in between. The staff counts letters; the keyboard counts keys. Keeping those two apart is most of what makes sharps and flats confusing later.

Now read it with your hands

A note appears. Play it. This is the whole skill in miniature — symbol in, key out — and it's the direction you're slowest in, so this is where the time should go.

When the three landmarks are landing without hesitation, add a step either side of each one — that's eight notes, and the point is that you find them by stepping from a landmark, not by naming them.

Check yourself

Between now and next time

Ten minutes. Two of the warm-up quiz above (it will keep re-testing lesson 1 until those facts are automatic), then the landmark drill until the median drops under two seconds. Then one thing away from the screen: look at your keyboard, close your eyes, and find Middle C, then Treble G, then Bass F by feel. That last exercise is aimed squarely at the gap you named — picturing the keyboard without looking at it.

Where this is going Für Elise opens on E and D♯ just above Middle C — a step and a half-step from a landmark you now know. Arctic Nights sits in E minor and leans on F♯ and D♯. Two more lessons of this and the opening of Für Elise stops being notation and starts being a thing your hands do.
Ask me things. Especially: why a step on the staff isn't always the next key along, what a ledger line actually is, or why middle C gets two addresses. And tell me your median on the landmark drill — if it's under two seconds we move to reading short phrases instead of single notes.
