Lesson 03 · Reading

A note is a fact. A line is a habit.

Last lesson you found single notes from a landmark. Now you stop going back to the landmark every time — and read each note from the one before it.

First — thirty seconds on last lesson

No keyboard. These are the facts the reading is built on; if any of them still needs a moment's thought, the line drills below will feel like work rather than reading. That's the tell that they aren't automatic yet.

The one idea in this lesson

Reading single notes, you had one move: find the nearest landmark, then step or skip to the note. That works, but it resets on every note — eyes back to the landmark, out to the note, back to the landmark. Good readers don't do that. Once they've placed the first note of a line, they read the second as a distance from the first, the third from the second, and so on. The landmark is where a line starts; the intervals carry you through the rest.

So the whole line is just the two moves you already know, chained:

The only two moves Step — line to the touching space, or space to the touching line. The next letter. Under the hand, a neighbouring finger.
Skip — line to the next line, or space to the next space. You jump one letter. Under the hand, a finger's gap.

A line of music is nothing but a string of these. Read the shape, not the names.

Here is a line to look at — not to name. See how it moves: up a step, up a step, down a step, then a skip up (notice it jumps from a space clear over the line to the next space). That contour is the thing to read.

Now read a line with your right hand

A line appears; a soft column marks the note you're on. Play it, and the column moves to the next. Play the whole line left to right. Stay in the treble staff, around Middle C and Treble G — the two landmarks you know — so the only new thing is carrying from note to note.

New here: the 💡 Hints button. Turn it on and every key you press pops onto the staff with its letter, then fades — right notes and wrong ones alike, so a miss shows you where the key you actually hit lives and what it's called. It's training wheels: leave it on while the positions are still landing, then switch it off, because reading is what you do when nothing is echoed back.

When steps are landing smoothly — now mix in skips. A skip looks like a bigger gap on the staff and feels like a bigger gap in your hand; the point of mixing them is that you have to tell them apart at a glance, which naming can't do fast enough.

Now the same thing, left hand

Down to the bass staff, around Bass F. Identical skill, other hand, other clef — and the clef is the point. The same shape on the page is a different set of keys down here, so you can't lean on "that pattern was C–D–E last time". You read the bass line as bass, from its own landmark.

Same move once the left hand is steady — mix skips into the bass too.

Why hands separately Reading two staves at once is a real skill, and it's a later one — trying it now just halves the attention each hand gets. Every good method reads hands separately first and joins them only when each is close to automatic. We'll join them at the chords lesson, where the left hand has a simpler job and there's a reason to.

Check yourself

Between now and next time

Ten minutes, most days. Run the treble line drill until notes-right holds around ninety percent and the median line drops under about four seconds, then the same for the bass. Do steps until they're dull before you add skips — bored is the target; bored means automatic. Once a hand is accurate, switch the drill to ⏱ sprint and read as many lines as you can in a minute: accuracy tells you that you can read it, but lines-per-minute is the number that has to climb, and watching it climb is the point. One thing away from the screen: open any easy piece, put a finger on the first note of a phrase, and read the rest as up/down, step/skip out loud without playing. Naming the contour is what you're training; the keys will follow.

Where this is going You now turn a written line into the right keys. What's missing is when — how long each note is held and where the beat falls. That's the next lesson: quarter, half, whole and eighth notes, counted against a pulse, laid straight onto lines you can already read. Pitch first, then time on top of it — which is the order that lets you actually play a phrase instead of spelling it.
Tell me your numbers. Notes-right and median line, each hand — that's what decides the next move. If skips are landing as easily as steps in both clefs, say so and we go to rhythm. If one hand is dragging, we stay and widen its range instead. And ask me things: what a ledger line really is once a line climbs off the staff, or why the bass clef seems to "lie" about where C is.
