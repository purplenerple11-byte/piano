Lesson 04 · Rhythm

One note is enough to make music

Every drill so far graded which key. This one grades when — and to do that properly, it only needs a single note.

Why one note

Your line reading sits at 84% right hand, 81% left. That is good progress and it is also the reason this lesson uses one note: if you were reading pitches and keeping time, a miss could be either, and you'd have no way to tell which to fix. Strip the pitch out and a bad score can only mean one thing.

This isn't a workaround. It's how the most recognisable piano hook of the last twenty years is built — the intro to Kanye West's Runaway is a single repeated note with enormous space around it. No chord, no melody. Everything that makes it work is timing and restraint. By the end of this page you'll have the skill that hook is made of.

How long a note lasts

A notehead's shape tells you its length. That's all notation is doing here: hollow lasts longer than filled, a stem halves it, a flag or beam halves it again.

Eighth — filled, flag or beam½1 &

Four beats to a bar is what 4/4 means, and it's what almost everything you want to play is in — including all four of your target pieces. Count out loud: "1 & 2 & 3 & 4 &". The numbers are the beats; the "&"s are the halves between them. Saying it aloud is not a beginner's crutch — it externalises the pulse so you can hear yourself drift.

The thing nobody says out loud A rest is not a pause in the music. It is a note you play silence on, and it lasts an exact length. Beginners rush rests because nothing is happening; the count keeps going anyway. In Runaway the rests are doing more work than the notes.

Hear the pulse first

Connect the piano, then play any key on every click. Not a tune — just the pulse. The drill grades how close you are in milliseconds, and tells you whether you're consistently early or late, which are different problems with different fixes.

Now a rhythm, not just a pulse

Same single note. Now the lengths change: two quarters, then two eighths (beamed together — that beam is what makes "3 &" visible at a glance), then a quarter. Count "1, 2, 3 &, 4" out loud before you play it.

Rests: the Runaway lesson

Here is the shape that hook is built from — a note, then a long silence, then the note again. Playing this well is entirely about not playing during the rest, and coming back in exactly on time.

Go and play the actual thing

Put the recording of Runaway on. The intro is one note, high up, with a lot of air around it. Find that note by ear — start anywhere in the top octave and hunt until it matches — then play along with the record.

Two skills at once, and both are on your mission: the timing you just drilled, and finding a pitch by ear, which is the half of your goal we haven't started yet. You don't need me to tell you the note. Hunting for it is the exercise.

If it sounds wrong Too low or too high is normal — move an octave and try again. When it's right it will stop sounding like "a note" and start sounding like the song. That click of recognition is the thing ear training is training.

Check yourself

Between now and next time

Ten minutes: two of the pulse drill, three on the rhythm with rests, and five playing along to Runaway. Keep the reading going too — a line or two of lesson 3 each day, weighted to the left hand, which is running 37% slower than your right and just needs volume.

Where this is going You can now read pitches and you can keep time. Next is putting both hands down together — the real wall, and the last thing standing between you and the opening of Für Elise.
Ask me things. Good ones after this: why 4/4 is written the way it is, what a dotted note does, or how to count triplets. And tell me your Avg off and Tendency on the rests drill — rushing versus dragging changes what I'd give you next.
