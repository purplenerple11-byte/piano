Lesson 01 · Geography

Name any key in under a second

The black keys are not obstacles. They are the map legend.

You know C chords, which means you know one shape in one place. The reason the rest of the keyboard feels far away is that you find notes by counting up from C. Counting is fine at three notes a minute and useless at three notes a second. This lesson replaces counting with recognition.

Here is the whole idea, and it is genuinely the whole idea:

The one thing Black keys repeat forever in a group of two and a group of three. C is the white key immediately left of the group of two. F is the white key immediately left of the group of three. Everything else is counted from one of those, never from the end of the keyboard.

That is why the pattern exists at all: seven white keys and five black keys per octave, and five will not split evenly into a symmetric pattern — so it splits 2 and 3, and that asymmetry is exactly what makes the keyboard readable by touch. A perfectly even keyboard would be unplayable in the dark.

Connect your keyboard

Plug the keyboard into the computer by USB and press connect. The drills below will then grade what you actually play, not what you click — which is the point, since the skill is in your hands, not your mouse.

No keyboard to hand? Everything still works — just click the on-screen keys instead.

The map

Play anything. It lights up. Find the group of two, then the group of three, then the group of two again — say "two, three, two, three" out loud as you move up. You are learning to see the grouping before you see any letter.

Why every black key has two names

A black key is named by the white key it leans on. Sharp (♯) means one key to the right; flat (♭) means one key to the left. The black key between D and E is therefore D♯ and E♭ — one key, two names, depending on which neighbour you came from. Two names is not a complication to memorise later; it is the fastest way to find the key now, because you can approach it from whichever side you already know.

Watch for There is no black key between E–F or between B–C. Those two pairs are the seams of the 2-group and 3-group. Nearly every beginner mistake on this keyboard is a note found by assuming a black key sits there.

Drill 1 · Name → key

A name appears. Play it, in any octave, as fast as you can. The clock starts the instant the name appears, so don't read it twice.

When black keys are landing near a second, run the same drill on everything — white and black interleaved. Switch this drill to all twelve keys.

Drill 2 · Key → name

Now the other direction. A key lights up; name it. Recognising a key and producing a key are two different skills, and only drilling both makes the map work in both directions — which is what sight reading will demand of you later.

Check yourself

No keyboard for this part — answer from memory. Getting it wrong here is more useful than getting it right with the keyboard in front of you.

What to do between sessions

Ten minutes is enough. Two minutes of Drill 1, two of Drill 2, then spend the rest playing a C chord and moving the whole shape up to sit on D, then E, then F — naming out loud each key you land on. It will sound wrong on some of them. That is a later lesson, and hearing why it sounds wrong is a head start on it.
