Lesson 05 · Coordination

Two hands, one moment

The wall everyone hits. It's smaller than it looks, because it isn't a reading problem.

Why this feels impossible at first

You can read a treble line. You can read a bass line. Put them on the page together and it falls apart — and the natural conclusion is that you need to get better at reading. You don't. Your reading is fine.

The trap is thinking of it as two things at once. Your attention can't split, so it flips between hands, and whichever hand isn't being watched drifts. The fix is to stop trying to split:

The one idea Hands together is not two parts played simultaneously. It is one part with two notes in it. When both hands land on the same beat, that is a single event — one gesture, one decision, two fingers arriving. Stop counting two things and start feeling one.

That's why the drill below doesn't just check whether you played the right notes. It measures how far apart your hands landed, in milliseconds. You can play every note correctly and still sound like two people — and accuracy alone would never show it.

The number to watch Under 50ms and a listener hears one sound. Past 120ms it's an audible flam — that slightly drunk, rolled quality where the hands don't agree. If you're flamming, the answer is never "try harder". It's slow down until they arrive together, then speed up.

Start with the easy shape: taking turns

Before both hands land at once, do the shape where they hand off to each other — right hand plays a few notes, then the left, then back. Only one hand is ever busy, so there's nothing to synchronise. What's new is that your eye has to notice which staff the next note is on and switch clefs mid-line.

This is not a warm-up exercise, by the way. It's the actual texture of the opening of Für Elise: the right hand plays its little E–D♯–E–D♯ figure, the left answers with a broken chord, and they barely overlap. Get comfortable here and that piece stops being theoretical.

Now both at once

Every column now wants a note from each hand. Play them in either order — the drill doesn't care which finger moves first, only how close together they arrive. It will tell you after each column.

Do this slowly. There is no clock and no score for speed here, deliberately. The only thing that matters is that the two notes become one event.

Three things that actually help

  1. Look at the gap, not the notes. Once you know the two notes, stop reading them and watch the distance between your hands. Coordination is a physical shape, not a pair of facts.
  2. Play it wrong on purpose. Deliberately land the left hand first, then deliberately the right. Being able to choose the error is how you stop making it by accident — you've moved it from something that happens to you to something you control.
  3. Say "down" out loud as they land. One word, one event. It is much harder to say a single word for two separate things, which is exactly the point.
Your timing is an advantage here You came out of lesson 4 at 20–28ms off the click, dead even. That's a strong internal pulse, and it's the thing hands-together is built on — you already know where "now" is, and you just need both hands to agree on it. This should come faster to you than it does to most people.

Check yourself

Between now and next time

Ten minutes: three on the alternating drill, five on both-hands-together, and two of lesson 3 with the left hand — still your slower one, and it will quietly limit everything here until it catches up.

And take the slow-tempo problem you spotted in lesson 4 seriously: run the rests drill at 60bpm and below, counting "1 & 2 &" out loud. Subdividing is what stops the rushing, and slow is the tempo hands-together is learned at.

Where this is going Once both hands agree on a moment, the next thing is worth playing rather than drilling: triads and the I–IV–V–vi progression, which is the fastest route to sounding like actual music — and the key that opens Say You Will, I Wonder and See Me Again.
Ask me things. Good ones here: why the left hand feels slower than the right even when it knows the notes, what to do when one hand knows a passage and the other doesn't, or how to practise a passage hands-separately without wasting the time.
