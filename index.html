Piano

From C chords to reading, chords in any key, and playing by ear.

Which device to use On a computer, in Chrome or Edge: the drills read your keyboard directly and grade what you actually play. This is the way to practise.

On iPad or iPhone: the lessons and reference sheets work fine — add this to your home screen and it opens like an app, which makes a good music stand next to the piano. But no iOS browser can read a MIDI keyboard (Web MIDI has never shipped in WebKit, and every iOS browser is required to use WebKit — so Chrome for iOS is no better than Safari). On iPad the drills work by tapping the on-screen keys. Useful for the naming drills; not a substitute for playing.

Safari and Firefox on desktop have no Web MIDI either — same tapping fallback.

Where this is going

The route, roughly in order. Each step exists because the one after it is impossible without it — not because it is the traditional order.

  1. Geography — the keys, by pattern rather than by counting. (Lesson 1)
  2. Staff landmarks + rhythm — a handful of anchor notes, everything else read as a distance from one; quarter/half/whole/eighth and how to count them.
  3. Single-hand reading — steps, then skips, hands separately. Easy daily sight-reading starts here and never stops.
  4. Chords and hands together — triads in C, G and F; the I–IV–V–vi progression; left hand from block to broken to Alberti. The fastest route to sounding like music.
  5. Keys and accidentals — circle of fifths, scales as geography rather than as exercises, reading in sharp and flat keys.
  6. Ear and repertoire — finding chord changes by ear, lead sheets, light improvising over a progression.
