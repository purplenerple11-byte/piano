Setting up the iPad

Your piano and your iPad get along fine — GarageBand reads it happily. The snag is narrower than it sounds: Safari refuses to hand MIDI to web pages. Apple declined the Web MIDI API in 2020 over fingerprinting concerns and has never shipped it, and because every iOS browser is required to use Apple's engine, Chrome for iOS is no better. So there is nothing to install into Safari — you need a browser that bridges MIDI itself.

One exists, it's free, and it is the only one: Web MIDI Browser. It reads every MIDI input iPadOS knows about — so you can feed it by cable or by Bluetooth. The cable is the more reliable of the two; pick whichever you prefer.

Option A — cable (fewer things to go wrong)

  1. Connect the piano to the iPad with a genuine Apple USB Camera Adapter (Lightning or USB-C, matching your iPad) and the piano's USB-B "printer" cable. It must be Apple's own adapter — clones often don't pass enough power. The FP-10 is class-compliant, so no driver is needed.
  2. Open Web MIDI Browser — not Safari.
  3. Load this site in it and open a lesson. There is nothing to "pair" over a cable; the piano just appears.

Option B — Bluetooth (no adapter, but one fiddly step)

  1. Turn the piano on with Bluetooth enabled. On the FP-10 it is on by default, and the [FUNCTION] button lights when a connection is live. The toggle is [FUNCTION] + C7♯ for on, [FUNCTION] + D7 for off.
  2. Open Web MIDI Browser — not Safari.
  3. Pair the piano using the app's own Bluetooth button. This is the step that catches everyone: Bluetooth MIDI instruments never show up in iOS Settings → Bluetooth. Pairing always happens inside the app that wants the MIDI. Roland's own manual pairs through the app's screen for the same reason.
  4. Then load this site in that browser and open a lesson.
Worth knowing Web MIDI Browser was last updated in 2016, so it may or may not behave on a current iPad. If anything looks wrong, open the connection test — it reports exactly what the browser can and can't see, which is far faster than guessing.

Also: a third-party browser can't "Add to Home Screen". So on iPad it's Safari for reading (install it to the home screen — it makes a good music stand) and Web MIDI Browser for graded drills. Two icons, two jobs.

Lessons

Lesson 01 Keyboard geography The 2-black / 3-black pattern as your map. Name any key — including every black key — in under a second, drilled in both directions against your actual playing. Lesson 02 Staff landmarks Three anchor notes — Treble G, Bass F, Middle C — and everything else read as a step or a skip from the nearest one. A note appears; you play it. No mnemonics. Lesson 03 Reading a line Stop returning to a landmark for every note: read each note as a step or skip from the one before. Whole lines now, played left to right, hands separately — right hand in treble, left in bass. Lesson 04 Rhythm Note values, counting, and rests — drilled against a click and graded in milliseconds, on a single note so timing is the only thing being measured. Ends on the Runaway intro. Lesson 05 Hands together The wall — and it isn't a reading problem. Hands take turns first, then land on the same moment, graded on how many milliseconds apart they arrive.

Where this is going

The route, roughly in order. Each step exists because the one after it is impossible without it — not because it is the traditional order.

  1. Geography — the keys, by pattern rather than by counting. (Lesson 1)
  2. Staff landmarks — a handful of anchor notes, everything else read as a distance from one. (Lesson 2)
  3. Single-hand reading — steps, then skips, hands separately; whole lines read one interval at a time. Easy daily sight-reading starts here and never stops. (Lesson 3)
  4. Rhythm — note values and counting, drilled against a click on a single note so timing is measured on its own. (Lesson 4)
  5. Hands together — two hands agreeing on one moment. (Lesson 5)
  6. Chords — triads in C, G and F; the I–IV–V–vi progression; left hand from block to broken to Alberti. The fastest route to sounding like music.
  7. Keys and accidentals — circle of fifths, scales as geography rather than as exercises, reading in sharp and flat keys.
  8. Ear and repertoire — finding chord changes by ear, lead sheets, light improvising over a progression.
